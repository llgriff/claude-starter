# Expressing Intent: How to Get What You Actually Want

The single biggest factor in whether AI helps you or wastes your time is how you express what you want. This isn't about prompt engineering tricks. It's about thinking clearly and communicating it.

## The Basics

**Say what you want, not how to do it.** "Build me a landing page for a dog walking business" is better than "create an HTML file with a header, three sections, and a footer using Tailwind CSS." Let the AI figure out the how. Jump in on the how when you have opinions about it.

**Context is everything.** "Write an email" is vague. "Write an email to my team announcing we're changing our standup time from 9am to 10am, keep it casual, we're a 5-person startup" gives the AI everything it needs to get it right on the first try.

**Constraints are your friend.** "Keep it under 200 words." "No bullet points." "Make it sound like a text message, not a corporate memo." Constraints produce better output than open-ended requests.

## Levels of Direction

**Level 1: "Do this."** You know exactly what you want. "Rename the `processData` function to `transformUserInput` and update all references." Clear, specific, fast.

**Level 2: "Figure this out."** You know the outcome but not the path. "This function is slow. Find out why and fix it." You're delegating the investigation and the solution.

**Level 3: "Think with me."** You're exploring. "I'm building a task manager. I'm not sure if I should use a database or just flat files. What are the tradeoffs for my situation?" You want a thinking partner, not an executor.

**Level 4: "Surprise me."** You want creative output. "Design a CLI that feels fun to use." Use `/beer` before these.

Most people default to Level 1 (micromanaging) or Level 3 (too vague). The sweet spot shifts depending on the task.

## Common Mistakes

**Over-specifying.** Telling the AI every detail of how to implement something. You hired an expert, let them be an expert. Tell them the outcome you need and the constraints that matter.

**Under-specifying.** "Make it better." Better how? Faster? Cleaner? More readable? More persuasive? Be specific about the dimension you care about.

**Asking for permission.** "Could you maybe help me with..." Just ask. "Help me write this proposal." Direct is faster.

**Not sharing context.** The AI doesn't know you're in a hurry, that this is for your boss, that you hate bullet points, that the last version was too formal. Everything that affects what "good" looks like needs to be said.

## The Feedback Loop

The first output is rarely perfect. That's fine. The magic is in the correction.

- "Too formal. Make it sound like I'm talking to a friend."
- "The second paragraph is the real point. Lead with that."
- "This is good but add a section about pricing."

Each correction teaches the AI about YOUR preferences. If you find yourself giving the same correction repeatedly, tell the AI to remember it: "Always write in a casual tone for me." That becomes a persistent preference.
